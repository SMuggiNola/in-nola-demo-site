Seanchas Mhuintir Mhongabháin

The Muggivan Seanchas

Every family carries a seanchas — its own holding of stories, kept and passed on. This is where ours begins: the scéalta of the Muggivan people, stretching from Derrycon above Mountshannon to seminaries in Ireland and priesthood in the United States — Patrick “Paddy” Muggivan and his son, John “JJ” Muggivan. Their scéalta are being gathered now.

Seanchas an Ainm — the story of the name

Ó Mongabháin — White-Mane

The name is Ó Mongabháin — White-Mane. An old Clare name, older than any record of who first wore it. The books say white hair. The family, who held a farm above Lough Derg at Derrycon for as long as paper exists and longer, say it in full: the horse’s white mane, the crest of a wave when the storm comes over the lake. The spelling changed every time a stranger held the pen; the sound and the meaning came down by mouth, through a man who signed with an X, and never changed at all.

The full telling — what’s documented, and what came down by mouth

I. The Irish name — documented

The surname is Ó Mongabháin, from the byname Mongbhán: mong (a mane, a head of hair — the horse’s word first, applied to people as metaphor) plus bán (white). “Descendant of White-Mane.” Woulfe’s 1923 dictionary records it as an old Clare surname, with the early anglicised form O Mongevaine appearing in English records centuries ago, and the modern scholarly gloss is “white hair.” It formed the standard way: one man carried the epithet, roughly a thousand years ago give or take centuries, and his grandchildren’s generation fixed it with Ó. Who he was is unrecorded and unrecoverable — no annal, no genealogy, no chief. An old name belonging to people history wasn’t written about.

II. The spellings — documented

Every English spelling is a stranger’s transcript of Irish sounds. West Clare settled on Mungovan / Mungavan (MUG-a-van); East Clare on Muggevin, then Muggivan — the 1901 census — then Mugivan in 1911. Same family, same farm, three spellings in sixty years, because the family couldn’t write and the pen kept guessing. What never changed was the mouth: MUGG-given, which the 1850s Muggevin spelling was already trying to capture. The pronunciation is the primary source; the paper is commentary.

III. The family’s meaning — oral tradition, and the treasure

The teaching received years ago: in the old Irish the name meant the horse’s white mane, and the crest of a wave — a whitecap. The books don’t have the wave; they stop at “white hair.” But the wave is linguistically genuine: mong extended in Irish to the crest of water, and whitecaps in living Irish are capaill bhána — white horses. The family version uses the word correctly in a sense the dictionaries dropped. It could only have crossed the language death — complete in that parish by 1901, the last real Irish in old Patrick’s generation — by being told, once, in English, in that house. Three or four tellings from the last Irish speakers to now.

IV. The lake — the setting that makes it cohere

Derrycon Lower runs to the shore of Lough Derg, the one water in that family’s world big enough to raise whitecaps, and it raises them fast when a storm crosses. A family named White-Mane, on that shore, in a language where the mane sat on the wave and the horses ran on the water. Whether the first Mongbhán was named for his hair or the water is unknowable — but the family that kept the name on that lake read it by the water in front of them, and that reading is what came down.

Every sentence above is either documented or marked as tradition — and the tradition is the better-preserved half.
